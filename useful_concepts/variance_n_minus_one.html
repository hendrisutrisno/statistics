Why the Sample Variance Uses \(n-1\)

See also

Back to Module 1 (Sample variance subsection): 1.6.2 Sample variance

Purpose

In Module 1, the sample variance is defined as

\[S^2=\frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(X_i-\bar{X})^2\]

This page explains why the denominator is \(n-1\) rather than \(n\). The explanation has two parts.

  1. A degrees of freedom argument: once the sample mean is fixed, only \(n-1\) deviations can vary independently.

  2. An unbiasedness argument: dividing by \(n-1\) makes \(S^2\) match the long-run variance level \(\sigma^2\) under i.i.d. sampling.

Two objects: population variance vs sample variance

Let \(X\) represent a population or process measurement.

  • The population variance \(\sigma^2\) is a parameter. It describes the long-run spread of the population distribution.

  • The sample variance \(S^2\) is a statistic. It is computed from a finite sample and changes from sample to sample.

The sample variance measures spread by using deviations from the sample mean. For each observation, define the deviation

\[d_i=X_i-\bar{X}\]

The quantity \(\sum_{i=1}^{n}d_i^2\) measures the total squared spread of the sample around its own center \(\bar{X}\). The denominator determines how we scale this total spread into an average-like measure.

Key constraint: deviations must sum to zero

The sample mean is

\[\bar{X}=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i\]

Now consider the sum of deviations from \(\bar{X}\)

\[\sum_{i=1}^{n}(X_i-\bar{X}) =\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i-n\bar{X}\]

Since \(n\bar{X}=\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i\), we obtain

\[\sum_{i=1}^{n}(X_i-\bar{X})=0\]

This is not an optional property. It is automatically true for any dataset once \(\bar{X}\) is computed.

Interpretation. The deviations around the sample mean must balance. If some observations are above \(\bar{X}\), then others must be below \(\bar{X}\) so that the total deviation is exactly zero.

Degrees of freedom: why only \(n-1\) can vary freely

Define the deviations \(d_i=X_i-\bar{X}\) for \(i=1,\dots,n\). From the previous section,

\[\sum_{i=1}^{n}d_i=0\]

This equation is a linear constraint that ties all \(n\) deviations together.

A useful way to see the consequence is to treat the first \(n-1\) deviations as free choices. Once \(d_1,\dots,d_{n-1}\) are fixed, the last deviation must satisfy the constraint, so it is forced to be

\[d_n=-\sum_{i=1}^{n-1}d_i\]

Therefore, only \(n-1\) deviations can vary independently. The remaining deviation is determined by the requirement that deviations sum to zero.

This is the meaning of “\(n-1\) degrees of freedom” in this setting: after estimating the center with \(\bar{X}\), the sample contains only \(n-1\) independent pieces of information about spread.

Example: the last deviation is determined

Suppose \(n=3\) and the deviations from the sample mean are \(d_1,d_2,d_3\). The constraint is

\[d_1+d_2+d_3=0\]

If \(d_1=2\) and \(d_2=-1\), then the last deviation must be

\[d_3=-(2+(-1))=-1\]

So the third deviation is not free once the first two are fixed.

What this example shows conceptually is that centering the data at \(\bar{X}\) removes one dimension of variability. One degree of freedom is used to locate the center, leaving \(n-1\) degrees of freedom for spread.

Unbiasedness: why \(n-1\) gives the correct long-run level

The degrees-of-freedom argument explains why \(n-1\) appears naturally when measuring spread around the sample mean. The second argument explains why \(n-1\) is also the correct scaling if the goal is to estimate \(\sigma^2\).

Assume an i.i.d. sample with

  • population mean \(\mu\)

  • population variance \(\sigma^2\)

A standard result is

\[\mathbb{E}\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^{n}(X_i-\bar{X})^2\right]=(n-1)\sigma^2\]

Interpretation. If sampling could be repeated many times, the average value of the total squared deviation around \(\bar{X}\) would be \((n-1)\sigma^2\), not \(n\sigma^2\). The reason is that using \(\bar{X}\) rather than \(\mu\) makes the deviations slightly smaller on average, because \(\bar{X}\) is chosen from the data to be the best-fitting center for that sample.

Dividing by \(n-1\) converts this expected total into the variance level

\[\mathbb{E}[S^2]=\sigma^2\]

So \(S^2\) is an unbiased estimator of \(\sigma^2\) under the i.i.d. model. In other words, \(S^2\) has the correct long-run target when the model assumptions hold.

A brief contrast: what if we divide by \(n\)

Sometimes we also see the alternative quantity

\[\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(X_i-\bar{X})^2\]

This quantity is the average squared deviation from the sample mean using \(n\) in the denominator. Under i.i.d. sampling, its expectation is

\[\mathbb{E}\!\left[\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(X_i-\bar{X})^2\right]=\frac{n-1}{n}\sigma^2\]

So it tends to underestimate \(\sigma^2\) on average for finite \(n\).

This does not make it useless. It is often used as a descriptive measure of within-sample spread, and it also appears naturally in some optimization settings. However, when the goal is inference about \(\sigma^2\), the \(n-1\) version is the standard choice because it removes this systematic downward bias.

Key takeaways

  • Deviations around the sample mean satisfy \(\sum_{i=1}^{n}(X_i-\bar{X})=0\)

  • This constraint means only \(n-1\) deviations can vary independently

  • Estimating the mean uses one degree of freedom, leaving \(n-1\) degrees of freedom for spread

  • Dividing by \(n-1\) makes \(S^2\) unbiased for \(\sigma^2\) under i.i.d. sampling
